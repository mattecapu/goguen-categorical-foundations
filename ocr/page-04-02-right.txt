The notion of a T-object, with its
arbitrary T and I, and associated structuré
category, is concrete, suggestive, and
useful, though much more general than any-
thing in the literature we know of. It is
closely related to ideas of Zadeh [10],
Messarovié [6], and Windeknecht [9], from
which we have drawn inspiration. We
believe it solves the problem of how to
introduce specific structures conveniently
into general systems theory. However,
systems of T-objects are more general, and
the completely arbitrary systems of the
next section are still more general.

3. Systems

A T-system should be a collection of T-
objects somehow connected together. Such a
thing is usually represented graphically
as a set of 'black boxes' with some inputs
and/or outputs connected to others. An
appropriate formalisation is a directed
graph with objects at nodes, and arrows
for input and/or output lines indicating
the flow of information (see Fig.3). For
highly interactive systems a distinction
between input and output may not make much
sense, so unordered graphs are appropriate.
There is also a need to introduce
'imaginary' objects which combine or split
inputs or output (the nodes labeled just -
in Fig.3). These objects may function
differently in different contexts; e.g.

as "OR's'' in logic circuits; as 'equali-
zers' of voltage levels or 'dividers' of
currents in electrical circuits; and as
'multipliers' or 'reproducers' of signals
in general systems diagrams. This can lead
to troublesome ambiguities. In addition,
rules are often imposed to prohibit diffi-
cult to interpret situations, such as
'dangling' inputs or outputs. Sometimes
'feedback' causes difficulties.

In this section we propose a formalism
for systems which seems simpler and more
natural, and has technical advantages.
